## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: Types of Essays (WAEC/NECO Writing Section)

**Definition**: An essay is a piece of formal or informal writing organized around a central idea

**The 5 Main Essay Types**:

| Type | Purpose | Key Signal Words | Common WAEC Topics |
|------|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Narrative** | Tell a story (real or imaginary) | "Write a story about…", "Narrate…" | An adventure, A memorable day |
| **Descriptive** | Create a vivid picture in words | "Describe…", "Write about the scene of…" | My town, A market scene |
| **Expository** | Explain / inform | "Explain how…", "Discuss…", "Write an article about…" | Effects of social media, Why students fail |
| **Argumentative / Persuasive** | Convince the reader | "Write a speech for/against…", "Argue…" | Boarding school vs day school |
| **Letter Writing** | Communicate formally or informally | "Write a letter to…" | Formal letter to a principal, informal letter to a friend |

---

### Concept 2: Essay Structure (For ALL Types)

**The Universal 5-Paragraph Structure**:
```
1. INTRODUCTION (1 paragraph)
   - Hook (attention-grabbing opening sentence)
   - Background/context
   - Thesis statement (your main point or argument)

2. BODY PARAGRAPH 1 (1 paragraph)
   - Topic sentence (main idea of this paragraph)
   - Supporting evidence / explanation / example
   - Linking sentence

3. BODY PARAGRAPH 2 (1 paragraph)
   - Topic sentence
   - Supporting evidence / explanation / example
   - Linking sentence

4. BODY PARAGRAPH 3 (1 paragraph) [optional for shorter essays]
   - Topic sentence
   - Supporting evidence / explanation / example
   - Linking sentence

5. CONCLUSION (1 paragraph)
   - Restate thesis (in different words)
   - Summary of key points
   - Closing thought / call to action (for argumentative)
```

**Word Count**: WAEC essays = approximately 450–500 words. NECO = 250–300 words.

### Concept 3: Letter Writing (High-Frequency Topic)

**Formal Letter Layout**:
```
Your address (top right, no name)          18 Bello Street
                                            Ikeja, Lagos State
                                            29 March, 2026

Recipient's address (top left)
The Principal
Sunshine Secondary School
Port Harcourt, Rivers State

Salutation                                  Dear Sir/Madam,

Subject heading (underlined)               RE: APPLICATION FOR ADMISSION

Body (3–4 paragraphs)
  - Para 1: State purpose of letter
  - Para 2+: Develop main points
  - Final para: Closing / call to action

Complimentary close                         Yours faithfully,

Signature & name (printed)                  Amina Yusuf
```

**Informal Letter Layout** (to a friend):
```
Your address (top right)
Date

Dear [First Name],

Opening: Greet / ask about their wellbeing
Body: Share news, respond to their letter, tell your story
Closing: Send regards to family, say goodbye

Your loving friend,
[Your first name only]
```

**Critical Differences**:
| Feature | Formal | Informal |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Salutation | Dear Sir/Madam / Dear Mr Adeyemi | Dear Chidi / Hi Ngozi |
| Sign off | Yours faithfully / Yours sincerely | Yours lovingly / Your friend |
| Language | Formal, no contractions | Conversational, contractions OK |
| Address | Both addresses shown | Only sender's address |

### Concept 4: Lexis & Structure – Vocabulary and Language Use

**Lexis** refers to vocabulary choice and word meaning in context

**Most Tested Lexis Topics in JAMB**:

**1. Antonyms** (opposites):
| Word | Antonym |
|------|---------|
| Benevolent | Malevolent |
| Verbose | Laconic / Concise |
| Ephemeral | Permanent / Eternal |
| Arduous | Easy / Effortless |
| Lucid | Obscure / Vague |

**2. Synonyms** (similar meaning):
| Word | Synonym |
|------|---------|
| Commence | Begin / Start |
| Obstinate | Stubborn / Tenacious |
| Meticulous | Careful / Precise |
| Alleviate | Reduce / Ease |
| Eminent | Distinguished / Notable |

**3. Idioms and Phrases** (WAEC/JAMB favourite):
| Idiom | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| Beat about the bush | Avoid the main point |
| Bite the bullet | Endure pain or hardship |
| Burn the midnight oil | Work or study late into the night |
| Kick the bucket | Die (informal) |
| Once in a blue moon | Very rarely |
| A blessing in disguise | Something good appearing bad at first |
| Hit the nail on the head | Be exactly right |
| Let the cat out of the bag | Reveal a secret accidentally |
| On the fence | Undecided |
| Spill the beans | Reveal secret information |

**4. Figures of Speech**:
| Figure | Definition | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Simile | Comparison using "like" or "as" | He runs *like the wind* |
| Metaphor | Direct comparison (no like/as) | Life is *a journey* |
| Personification | Giving human qualities to non-human | *The sun smiled* down on us |
| Hyperbole | Extreme exaggeration | I've told you *a million times* |
| Alliteration | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | *Peter Piper picked* pickled peppers |
| Oxymoron | Contradiction in terms | *Deafening silence*, *Living death* |
| Irony | Saying the opposite of what is meant | "What beautiful weather!" (said in a storm) |

### Concept 5: Oral English (Phonetics) – JAMB-Tested

**Vowel Sounds** (12 Pure Vowels + 8 Diphthongs in Standard British English):

**Key JAMB-Tested Vowel Patterns**:
| Sound | Examples |
|-------|---------|
| /iː/ (long ee) | seat, beat, feet, key |
| /ɪ/ (short i) | sit, bit, fit, big |
| /æ/ | cat, bat, man, can |
| /ɑː/ (long a) | car, bar, farm, heart |
| /ɒ/ (short o) | hot, got, lock |
| /ɔː/ (long aw) | law, saw, court, more |
| /ʌ/ | cup, bus, but, sun |
| /uː/ (long oo) | food, moon, rule |
| /ʊ/ (short oo) | book, look, good, foot |
| /ɜː/ (er) | bird, word, turn, learn |
| /ə/ (schwa) | about, the, again |

**Consonant Contrasts Commonly Tested**:
- /p/ vs /b/: *pin* vs *bin*
- /s/ vs /z/: *seal* vs *zeal*
- /θ/ vs /ð/: *think* vs *this*
- /ʃ/ vs /tʃ/: *shoe* vs *chew*

**Stress Patterns** (JAMB favourite):
```
Noun vs Verb pairs (stress shifts):
  REcord (noun) → reCORD (verb)
  PROtest (noun) → proTEST (verb)
  PERmit (noun) → perMIT (verb)
  PREsent (noun/adj) → preSENT (verb)
  CONtent (noun) → conTENT (adj)
```

**Rhyme** (same vowel + consonant sounds):
- fame / game / same / name all rhyme with /eɪm/
- JAMB question type: "Which word does NOT rhyme with…?"

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Idiom Meaning)
**Q**: What does the expression "burn the midnight oil" mean?

**Options**:
A) Set fire to an oil lamp
B) Work or study very late at night ← **CORRECT**
C) Waste energy on unimportant things
D) Cause a fire accident

**Explanation**:
- Before electricity, students used oil lamps at night
- "Burning midnight oil" = staying up late to work or study
- Context clue: exams are near → likely studying hard

**Key Concept**: Learn idioms as fixed units; the literal meaning is irrelevant

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Antonym)
**Q**: What is the antonym of "verbose"?

**Options**:
A) Talkative
B) Fluent
C) Laconic ← **CORRECT**
D) Eloquent

**Explanation**:
- Verbose = using more words than necessary (overly wordy)
- Laconic = using very few words; brief and concise
- Talkative and eloquent are closer in meaning to verbose
- Fluent refers to smoothness of speech, not quantity

**Key Concept**: Antonyms are opposite in meaning, not just different in meaning

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Formal Letter)
**Q**: Which of the following closings is appropriate for a formal letter addressed to someone you know by name?

**Options**:
A) Yours faithfully
B) Yours lovingly
C) Yours sincerely ← **CORRECT**
D) Your friend

**Explanation**:
- "Dear Mr Adeola" (named recipient) → close with "Yours sincerely"
- "Dear Sir/Madam" (unnamed recipient) → close with "Yours faithfully"
- "Yours lovingly" and "Your friend" are for informal letters only
- This rule is tested repeatedly in WAEC/JAMB

**Key Concept**: Named recipient → Yours sincerely; Unnamed → Yours faithfully

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Stress Pattern)
**Q**: In which of these words is the stress on the SECOND syllable?

**Options**:
A) REcord (noun)
B) PROtest (noun)
C) reCORD (verb) ← **CORRECT**
D) PERmit (noun)

**Explanation**:
- "record" as a noun: REcord (stress on 1st syllable)
- "record" as a verb: reCORD (stress on 2nd syllable)
- Same pattern: PROtest (n) → proTEST (v); PERmit (n) → perMIT (v)
- JAMB always tests noun/verb stress-shift pairs

**Key Concept**: Noun = stress on 1st syllable; Verb = stress on 2nd syllable (for paired words)

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Figure of Speech)
**Q**: "The soldiers marched for a million miles" is an example of:

**Options**:
A) Simile
B) Personification
C) Irony
D) Hyperbole ← **CORRECT**

**Explanation**:
- Hyperbole = extreme exaggeration to make a point
- Soldiers clearly didn't march a million miles (impossible)
- The exaggeration emphasises how far and long they marched
- Simile needs "like" or "as"; Personification gives human traits to non-humans; Irony says the opposite of what is meant

**Key Concept**: Hyperbole uses impossible exaggeration to intensify meaning

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN ENGLISH SS3

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| "Yours faithfully" after "Dear Mr X" | You've named the recipient | Named person → "Yours sincerely" |
| Using informal language in a formal essay | Registers must match | Formal essay: no contractions, no slang |
| Treating simile and metaphor as identical | Both compare, but differently | Simile uses like/as; Metaphor is a direct identity |
| Stressing verbs like nouns | "PERmit me" | As a verb: "perMIT me" (stress shifts to 2nd syllable) |
| Confusing antonym with a word that's just different | "Hot" → "big" is not an antonym | Antonym must be directly opposite: "Hot" → "Cold" |

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **Formal letter sign-off rule**
- "Dear Sir/Madam" → Yours faithfully
- "Dear Mr/Mrs [Name]" → Yours sincerely

**Tip 2**: **Identify figures of speech by their key features**
- Simile: like/as; Metaphor: is/was (no like/as); Personification: human verb + non-human subject

**Tip 3**: **Stress shift: noun stress on 1st syllable, verb on 2nd**
- Applies to: record, permit, protest, present, content, conduct, object, project, produce

**Tip 4**: **Idioms: the literal meaning is always a distractor option**
- Always choose the figurative/idiomatic meaning, not the word-for-word reading

**Tip 5**: **Essay introduction must have a thesis statement**
- Examiners look for: Hook → Background → Clear thesis/argument

## 8. REAL WAEC/JAMB SS3 ENGLISH PATTERNS

**Pattern 1**: "Which sentence contains an example of [figure of speech]?"
- Identify the key feature: like/as, human verb, exaggeration, contradiction

**Pattern 2**: "The word ___ has the same vowel sound as…"
- Focus on the vowel sound, not the spelling

**Pattern 3**: "Which word does NOT rhyme with the others?"
- Match vowel + final consonant sounds

**Pattern 4**: "The expression '___' means…"
- Choose the idiomatic meaning; reject the literal distractor

**Pattern 5**: "Which of the following is the correct sign-off for a formal letter addressed to Dear Mr Adeyemi?"
- Yours sincerely (named) vs Yours faithfully (unnamed)
