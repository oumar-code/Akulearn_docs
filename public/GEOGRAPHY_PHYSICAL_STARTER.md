## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: Rocks and Their Classification

**Definition**: Rocks are naturally occurring solid masses of minerals or mineralised matter forming the Earth's crust

**The Three Major Rock Types**:

#### 1. Igneous Rocks
```
Formation: Formed by cooling and solidification of molten rock (magma or lava)

Two types:
  Intrusive (Plutonic): Magma cools SLOWLY underground
    → Large crystals formed
    → Examples: Granite, Gabbro, Diorite
    → Used in: Construction, road surfaces

  Extrusive (Volcanic): Lava cools QUICKLY on the surface
    → Small crystals or glassy texture
    → Examples: Basalt, Pumice, Obsidian
    → Used in: Filtration (pumice), polishing
```

#### 2. Sedimentary Rocks
```
Formation: Formed by accumulation and compaction of sediments over time
           (weathered material deposited in layers)

Three types:
  Mechanically formed: From compressed rock fragments
    → Examples: Sandstone, Shale, Conglomerate, Mudstone

  Organically formed: From remains of living organisms
    → Examples: Coal, Limestone, Chalk

  Chemically formed: From chemical precipitation
    → Examples: Rock salt, Gypsum, Dolomite

Key Features:
  - Occur in LAYERS (strata)
  - May contain FOSSILS
  - Usually the SOFTEST rock type
  - Cover about 75% of Earth's surface
```

#### 3. Metamorphic Rocks
```
Formation: Igneous or sedimentary rocks transformed by HEAT and/or PRESSURE

Original Rock → Metamorphic Rock:
  Granite    → Gneiss
  Limestone  → Marble
  Sandstone  → Quartzite
  Shale      → Slate
  Coal       → Graphite / Diamond

Key Features:
  - Very HARD (harder than original)
  - May show banding or foliation
  - Found near mountain belts (zones of high pressure)
```

**Rock Cycle**:
```
Igneous → (weathering/erosion) → Sedimentary
        → (heat & pressure)   → Metamorphic
Metamorphic → (melting) → Magma → Igneous
```

### Concept 2: Weather and Climate

**Weather**: Atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time (short-term)
**Climate**: Average atmospheric conditions of a place over a long period (30+ years)

**Elements of Weather/Climate** (measured at weather stations):
| Element | Instrument | Unit |
|---------|-----------|------|
| Temperature | Thermometer | °C |
| Rainfall | Rain gauge | mm |
| Atmospheric pressure | Barometer / Barograph | millibars (mb) |
| Humidity | Hygrometer | % |
| Wind speed | Anemometer | km/h or knots |
| Wind direction | Wind vane | Compass direction |
| Sunshine | Campbell-Stokes sunshine recorder | Hours |
| Cloud cover | Nephoscope / eye estimation | Oktas (0–8) |

**Factors Affecting Climate**:
1. **Latitude** – areas near equator are hotter (perpendicular solar radiation)
2. **Altitude** – temperature decreases by ~6.5°C per 1,000 m rise (lapse rate)
3. **Distance from the sea** – coastal areas have moderate climate; interior = extreme (continentality)
4. **Prevailing winds** – windward side = more rain; leeward = rain shadow
5. **Ocean currents** – warm currents increase temperature and rainfall; cold currents reduce both
6. **Vegetation** – forests increase humidity and rainfall through transpiration

**Climate Zones of Africa**:
| Zone | Location | Rainfall | Temperature |
|------|---------|----------|------------|
| Equatorial | Within 5° of equator (Congo, parts of Nigeria) | Heavy, year-round (2000mm+) | Always hot (~27°C) |
| Tropical (Guinea) | 5°–15° of equator | Seasonal (1000–2000mm) | Hot, two wet seasons |
| Sudan (Sub-tropical) | 15°–20° of equator | Low, short wet season | Hot, dry most of year |
| Sahel/Desert | 20°+ | Very low (<250mm) | Extreme heat by day, cold at night |
| Mediterranean | 30°–40° latitude | Rain in winter, dry summer | Warm; mild winters |

### Concept 3: Map Reading and Interpretation

**Definition**: A map is a scaled representation of the Earth's surface

**Types of Maps**:
| Type | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Topographic (Ordnance Survey) | Shows relief features (hills, valleys, rivers) |
| Political | Shows national/state boundaries |
| Thematic | Shows specific data (rainfall, population density) |
| Road map | Shows transport networks |
| Sketch map | Quick, not to scale |

**Scale**:
```
Scale = Map distance / Actual distance

Types of scale:
  Statement scale: "1 cm represents 5 km"
  Linear/Graphic scale: A bar showing distances
  Representative fraction (RF): 1:50,000 (1 cm = 50,000 cm = 500 m)

To find actual distance:
  Actual = Map distance × Denominator of RF
  Example: 2 cm on a 1:50,000 map = 2 × 50,000 = 100,000 cm = 1 km
```

**Contour Lines** (showing relief/elevation):
```
- Lines joining points of equal altitude
- Closely spaced contours = STEEP slope
- Widely spaced contours = GENTLE slope
- Concentric (V or U shapes):
    V-shape pointing upstream = VALLEY / RIVER
    V-shape pointing downslope = RIDGE / SPUR
- Circular contours:
    Highest number inside = HILL
    Lowest number inside = DEPRESSION

Key contour features:
  Hill        → Circular contours, numbers increase inward
  Valley      → V-shapes with river along the axis
  Ridge/Spur  → V-shapes pointing downhill, into the valley
  Col/Saddle  → Depression between two hilltops
  Cliff       → Contour lines touching or very close together
```

**Grid References**:
```
Four-figure grid reference: identifies a grid square
  e.g., 3754 → Easting 37, Northing 54

Six-figure grid reference: identifies a specific point within the square
  e.g., 374542 → more precise location

Rule: "Walk before you climb" (read Easting before Northing)
```

**Bearing**:
```
Bearing = direction measured clockwise from North (0°–360°)
  North = 000°
  East  = 090°
  South = 180°
  West  = 270°

Compass directions:
  NE = 045°, SE = 135°, SW = 225°, NW = 315°
```

### Concept 4: Population Geography

**Population Distribution**: How people are spread across an area

**Population Density**:
```
Population Density = Total Population / Area (km²)
```

**Factors Favouring High Population Density**:
- Fertile agricultural land (e.g., Nile Valley, coastal plains)
- Temperate/moderate climate
- Access to water
- Good transport links
- Availability of jobs (urban areas)

**Factors Favouring Low Population Density**:
- Extreme climate (desert, arctic)
- Rugged terrain (high mountains)
- Dense forests (Amazon, Congo)
- Disease-prone areas

**Urbanisation**: The increasing proportion of a population living in urban areas

**Problems of Rapid Urbanisation**:
- Overcrowding and slums
- Unemployment
- Poor sanitation and water supply
- Traffic congestion
- Crime and social unrest

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Rock Classification)
**Q**: Marble is a metamorphic rock formed from which rock?

**Options**:
A) Granite
B) Sandstone
C) Limestone ← **CORRECT**
D) Basalt

**Explanation**:
- Metamorphic rocks are transformations of existing rocks under heat and pressure
- Limestone → Marble (metamorphic); Sandstone → Quartzite; Granite → Gneiss
- Marble is used in building, sculptures, and floors

**Key Concept**: Remember transformation pairs: Limestone→Marble, Sandstone→Quartzite, Granite→Gneiss

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Map Scale)
**Q**: On a map with scale 1:100,000, two towns are 4 cm apart. What is the actual distance in km?

**Options**:
A) 4 km ← **CORRECT**
B) 40 km
C) 400 m
D) 0.4 km

**Solution**:
```
Actual distance = Map distance × denominator
                = 4 cm × 100,000
                = 400,000 cm
                = 4,000 m
                = 4 km
```

**Key Concept**: Actual = map distance × RF denominator; convert cm to km (÷ 100,000)

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Contour Interpretation)
**Q**: On a topographic map, closely-spaced contour lines indicate:

**Options**:
A) A gentle slope
B) A flat surface
C) A steep slope ← **CORRECT**
D) A river valley

**Explanation**:
- Closely-spaced contours = elevation changes rapidly over a short horizontal distance = steep slope
- Widely-spaced = gradual change = gentle slope
- A river valley is shown by V-shapes pointing upstream, not just spacing

**Key Concept**: Contour spacing = slope steepness; close = steep; wide = gentle

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Climate)
**Q**: The Equatorial climate is characterised by:

**Options**:
A) Low rainfall and extreme temperatures
B) Rainfall only in the wet season
C) Heavy rainfall throughout the year and consistently high temperatures ← **CORRECT**
D) Dry summers and wet winters

**Explanation**:
- Equatorial climate: within 5° of equator
- Temperature: always high (~27°C); no cold season
- Rainfall: heavy and year-round (>2000 mm/year)
- Option B describes tropical (Guinea climate); Option D is Mediterranean; Option A is desert

**Key Concept**: Equatorial = hot + wet all year; no dry season

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Grid Reference and Bearing)
**Q**: What is the bearing of point B (located directly south-west of point A)?

**Options**:
A) 045°
B) 135°
C) 225° ← **CORRECT**
D) 315°

**Explanation**:
- Bearings are measured clockwise from North (0°/360°)
- North = 000°; East = 090°; South = 180°; West = 270°
- South-West = midway between South (180°) and West (270°) = 225°
- Note: "B is south-west of A" means you travel from A towards the south-west to reach B → bearing from A to B = 225°

**Key Concept**: SW = 225°; NE = 045°; SE = 135°; NW = 315°

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN GEOGRAPHY

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Confusing weather and climate | Used interchangeably | Weather = short-term (today); Climate = long-term average (30 years) |
| Saying limestone is metamorphic | Limestone is sedimentary | Limestone → metamorphic = MARBLE |
| Reading grid references as Northing before Easting | Reversal of axes | "Walk before you climb": Easting (x) before Northing (y) |
| Confusing closely-spaced contours with a valley | Spacing and shape are different features | Closely-spaced = steep; V-shape pattern = valley |
| Forgetting to convert cm to km in scale problems | Answer left in cm | Divide by 100,000 to convert cm to km |
