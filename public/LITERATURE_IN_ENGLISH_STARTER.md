## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: Prose Fiction – Elements and Analysis

**Definition**: Prose is written language in its ordinary form, without metrical structure; prose fiction tells imaginary (or fictionalised real) stories

**Elements of a Novel or Short Story**:

| Element | Definition | Questions to Ask |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Plot** | The sequence of events | What happens? In what order? |
| **Character** | The people in the story | Who are they? How do they change? |
| **Setting** | Time and place of the story | Where? When? What atmosphere? |
| **Theme** | Central idea or message | What is the story ABOUT? |
| **Conflict** | Struggle driving the plot | Man vs Man, vs Nature, vs Society, vs Self |
| **Point of View** | Narrative perspective | 1st person (I), 3rd person (he/she/they) |
| **Style** | Author's use of language | Formal? Colloquial? Figurative? |
| **Mood/Tone** | Author's attitude; emotional atmosphere | Ironic? Sad? Comic? Suspenseful? |

**Types of Narrative Point of View**:
| POV | Description | Example |
|-----|-----------|---------|
| First person | Narrator is a character in the story; uses "I" | "I walked into the room" |
| Third person omniscient | Narrator knows all characters' thoughts | "She thought he was lying, but he wasn't" |
| Third person limited | Narrator follows only one character's perspective | "He saw the door open" (narrator = he only) |

**Types of Conflict**:
- **Man vs Man**: Character against another character
- **Man vs Society**: Character against social norms or institutions
- **Man vs Nature**: Character against natural forces (storm, disease)
- **Man vs Self**: Internal psychological struggle

### Concept 2: Poetry – Form, Structure, and Devices

**Definition**: Poetry is imaginative literature that uses rhythmic, compressed language to evoke emotion and meaning

**Elements of a Poem**:
| Element | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Stanza** | Group of lines (like a paragraph in prose) | A 4-line stanza = a "quatrain" |
| **Line** | Single row of text | "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" |
| **Rhyme scheme** | Pattern of rhyming line endings | ABAB, AABB, ABCB |
| **Rhythm** | Musical beat created by stressed/unstressed syllables | |
| **Imagery** | Language that appeals to the senses | "The golden sun sank into the sea" |
| **Persona** | The voice speaking in the poem (not always the poet) | |

**Types of Poems**:
| Type | Features | Examples |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Lyric** | Short, personal, emotional; expresses feelings | Sonnets, odes, elegies |
| **Narrative** | Tells a story | Ballads, epics |
| **Dramatic** | Spoken by a character in a dramatic situation | Dramatic monologue |
| **Satirical** | Criticises society or individuals using irony/humour | |
| **Didactic** | Intended to teach a lesson | |

**Common Poetic Forms**:
- **Sonnet**: 14 lines; usually ABAB CDCD EFEF GG (Shakespearean) or ABBA ABBA CDC DCD (Petrarchan)
- **Ode**: Formal, long lyric praising a person or thing
- **Elegy**: Poem of mourning (for the dead)
- **Ballad**: Narrative poem with a refrain; often set to music

**Rhyme Scheme Analysis**:
```
"Roses are red" → A
"Violets are blue" → B
"Sugar is sweet" → C
"And so are you" → B

Rhyme scheme = ABCB
```

### Concept 3: Drama – Elements and Conventions

**Definition**: Drama is a literary composition designed to be performed by actors; tells a story through dialogue and action

**Elements of Drama**:
| Element | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Plot** | Sequence of events (exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, denouement) |
| **Character** | Protagonist, antagonist, round/flat characters |
| **Setting** | Stage directions indicate where/when scenes take place |
| **Dialogue** | Spoken words between characters |
| **Conflict** | Central struggle (same types as prose) |
| **Theme** | Central idea or message |
| **Stage directions** | Instructions in the text for actors, lighting, movement |

**Dramatic Structure** (Freytag's Pyramid):
```
          CLIMAX (highest tension)
         /         \
Rising action    Falling action
       /                 \
EXPOSITION         DENOUEMENT/RESOLUTION
(Introduction)        (Outcome)
```

**Types of Drama**:
| Type | Features | Examples |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Tragedy** | Hero has a fatal flaw; ends in death or great suffering | Hamlet, Macbeth; Wole Soyinka's *Death and the King's Horseman* |
| **Comedy** | Light-hearted; ends happily; often satirises society | *A Midsummer Night's Dream* |
| **Tragicomedy** | Elements of both tragedy and comedy | Some Nigerian plays |
| **Melodrama** | Exaggerated emotions; clear good vs evil | |

**Important Terms**:
- **Soliloquy**: A character speaks thoughts aloud alone on stage (reveals inner feelings)
- **Aside**: A character speaks directly to the audience; other characters don't hear
- **Monologue**: Long speech by one character
- **Dialogue**: Conversation between two or more characters

### Concept 4: Literary Devices (Most Tested in WAEC/JAMB)

**Definition**: Techniques writers use to convey meaning, create effect, or add beauty

| Device | Definition | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Simile** | Comparison using like/as | "He is brave *as a lion*" |
| **Metaphor** | Direct comparison; states one thing IS another | "Life is a *journey*" |
| **Personification** | Giving human qualities to non-human things | "*The sun smiled* down on us" |
| **Hyperbole** | Extreme exaggeration | "I've told you *a million times*" |
| **Irony** | Saying the opposite of what is meant | Saying "Wonderful!" when something goes wrong |
| **Alliteration** | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | "*Peter Piper* picked peppers" |
| **Assonance** | Repetition of vowel sounds | "*The rain in Spain stays mainly* in the plain" |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Words that sound like what they describe | "The *buzz* of bees"; "*Crash*! The glass fell" |
| **Symbolism** | An object, person, or event that represents something else | A dove = peace; darkness = evil/ignorance |
| **Paradox** | A statement that seems contradictory but reveals truth | "The more you know, the more you know you don't know" |
| **Oxymoron** | Two contradictory terms placed together | "*Deafening silence*"; "*Living death*" |
| **Flashback** | Scene that returns to an earlier time | Character remembers the past |
| **Foreshadowing** | Hints about future events | A storm brewing before disaster strikes |

**Tone vs Mood**:
- **Tone** = the author's/poet's attitude toward the subject (e.g., sarcastic, celebratory, melancholic)
- **Mood** = the emotional atmosphere experienced by the READER (e.g., tense, joyful, eerie)

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Literary Device)
**Q**: "The wind whispered through the trees" is an example of:

**Options**:
A) Simile
B) Hyperbole
C) Personification ← **CORRECT**
D) Onomatopoeia

**Explanation**:
- "Whispered" is a human action → attributed to the wind (non-human) → personification
- Simile needs "like" or "as"; Hyperbole needs extreme exaggeration
- Onomatopoeia would be a sound-imitating word like "rustle" or "swoosh"

**Key Concept**: Personification = human action or quality given to a non-human subject

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Drama Terms)
**Q**: A speech delivered by a character alone on stage, revealing inner thoughts, is called a:

**Options**:
A) Dialogue
B) Aside
C) Soliloquy ← **CORRECT**
D) Monologue

**Explanation**:
- Soliloquy = character is ALONE, thinking aloud; no other character hears
- Aside = brief remark to the audience; other characters present but cannot hear
- Monologue = long speech by one person, but other characters can be present
- Dialogue = conversation between two or more characters

**Key Concept**: Soliloquy = alone + thinking aloud; Aside = brief private remark to audience

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Rhyme Scheme)
**Q**: Identify the rhyme scheme of this stanza:
*"Twinkle, twinkle, little star,* (line 1)
*How I wonder what you are!* (line 2)
*Up above the world so high,* (line 3)
*Like a diamond in the sky.* (line 4)"

**Options**:
A) ABAB
B) AABB ← **CORRECT**
C) ABBA
D) ABCB

**Explanation**:
- Line 1 (star) → A; Line 2 (are) → A (rhymes with star)
- Line 3 (high) → B; Line 4 (sky) → B (rhymes with high)
- Pattern = AABB

**Key Concept**: Label each line by its end rhyme sound; first sound = A, second new sound = B, etc.

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Conflict Type)
**Q**: In a story where the main character battles depression and self-doubt, the type of conflict present is:

**Options**:
A) Man vs Man
B) Man vs Society
C) Man vs Nature
D) Man vs Self ← **CORRECT**

**Explanation**:
- The struggle is internal (within the character's own mind)
- Man vs Self = internal psychological conflict (self-doubt, moral dilemma, grief)
- Man vs Man = conflict with another person
- Man vs Society = conflict with social norms, systems, or institutions

**Key Concept**: Internal struggle (depression, guilt, temptation) = Man vs Self

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Theme Analysis)
**Q**: In a novel where a character rises from poverty through perseverance but loses his moral values, the most appropriate theme is:

**Options**:
A) The joy of hard work
B) The inevitability of failure
C) The conflict between material success and moral integrity ← **CORRECT**
D) The importance of education

**Explanation**:
- Theme goes deeper than plot events → it is the central MESSAGE or truth
- The character succeeds materially but is corrupted morally → theme = tension between success and integrity
- This is a common theme in African novels (Things Fall Apart, The Beautyful Ones Are Not Yet Born)

**Key Concept**: Theme = the central idea/message the author wants to communicate about human experience

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN LITERATURE

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Confusing simile and metaphor | Both make comparisons | Simile uses like/as; Metaphor is a direct statement of identity |
| Confusing soliloquy with monologue | Both are long speeches | Soliloquy = alone on stage; Monologue = long speech (others may be present) |
| Saying the poet and the persona are the same | Not always true | The speaker in a poem is the PERSONA, not necessarily the poet |
| Describing plot instead of theme | Asked for "what the novel is about" | Theme = message/idea; Plot = events that happen |
| Confusing mood and tone | Both relate to feeling | Tone = author's attitude; Mood = reader's emotional experience |

## 4. KEY DIAGRAMS (To Understand)

### Diagram 1: Freytag's Pyramid (Dramatic Structure)
```
               ● CLIMAX
              / \
    Rising   /   \  Falling
    Action  /     \  Action
           /       \
──────────/─────────\──────────
Exposition           Denouement
(Beginning)          (Resolution/End)
```

### Diagram 2: Rhyme Scheme Labelling
```
"Roses are red"   → A  (first sound)
"My bike is new"  → B  (new sound)
"Sugar is sweet"  → C  (new sound)
"And so are you"  → B  ("you" rhymes with "new")

= ABCB rhyme scheme
```

### Diagram 3: Literary Devices Quick Reference
```
Comparison:
  Like/As → SIMILE        ("He fights like a lion")
  Is/Was  → METAPHOR      ("He is a lion in battle")

Non-human + human trait → PERSONIFICATION ("The sea roared angrily")
Exaggeration → HYPERBOLE ("I waited for a million years")
Sound = meaning → ONOMATOPOEIA ("Buzz", "Crash", "Hiss")
Opposites together → OXYMORON ("Bittersweet", "Deafening silence")
Opposite of what is meant → IRONY ("Oh great, another problem!")
```

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **Always identify the KEY WORD in literary device questions**
- "Like" or "as" → Simile; Human verb + non-human → Personification; Extreme exaggeration → Hyperbole

**Tip 2**: **Soliloquy = ALONE; Aside = brief to audience; Monologue = long speech (others present)**
- These three are consistently confused in WAEC/JAMB

**Tip 3**: **Theme is NOT the same as the plot**
- Plot = what happens; Theme = what the story MEANS or teaches

**Tip 4**: **Tone = author's attitude; Mood = reader's feeling**
- A dark tone creates a gloomy mood; an ironic tone creates a wry/satirical mood

**Tip 5**: **Rhyme scheme: label each line by its END sound; first new sound = A, second = B, etc.**
- Read aloud if needed; trust your ear

## 8. REAL WAEC/JAMB LITERATURE PATTERNS

**Pattern 1**: "The expression '___' is an example of…"
- Identify the literary device from the underlined expression

**Pattern 2**: "What is the theme of the passage/poem/play?"
- Central message/idea (not the plot summary)

**Pattern 3**: "The conflict in this passage is best described as…"
- Man vs Man/Society/Nature/Self

**Pattern 4**: "A character who speaks alone on stage to reveal inner thoughts is using a…"
- Soliloquy

**Pattern 5**: "The mood of the poem is best described as…"
- Tone/mood question: sad, hopeful, ironic, celebratory, melancholic, etc.
