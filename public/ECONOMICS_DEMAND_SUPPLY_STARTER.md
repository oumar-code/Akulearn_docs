## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: Demand

**Definition**: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at various prices, over a given period of time

**Law of Demand**: As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship), ceteris paribus (all other things equal)

**Demand Schedule**:
| Price (₦) | Quantity Demanded |
|-----------|------------------|
| 100 | 50 units |
| 80 | 70 units |
| 60 | 90 units |
| 40 | 120 units |

**Demand Curve**: Slopes downward from left to right (negative slope)

**Factors That Shift the Demand Curve** (Non-price factors):
1. **Income of consumers** – rise in income increases demand for normal goods
2. **Price of related goods** – substitutes: if Pepsi price rises, Coke demand rises; Complements: if petrol price rises, car demand falls
3. **Tastes and preferences** – fashion trends change demand
4. **Population size** – more people = higher demand
5. **Consumer expectations** – if price expected to rise, buy more now
6. **Government policies** – taxes, subsidies

**Types of Demand**:
- **Individual demand**: One consumer's demand
- **Market demand**: Sum of all individual demands
- **Joint/Complementary demand**: Goods demanded together (car + fuel)
- **Composite/Competing demand**: One good with multiple uses (milk → cheese, butter, cream)

---

### Concept 2: Supply

**Definition**: The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various prices, over a given period of time

**Law of Supply**: As price increases, quantity supplied increases (direct/positive relationship), ceteris paribus

**Supply Schedule**:
| Price (₦) | Quantity Supplied |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 40 | 20 units |
| 60 | 40 units |
| 80 | 60 units |
| 100 | 80 units |

**Supply Curve**: Slopes upward from left to right (positive slope)

**Factors That Shift the Supply Curve** (Non-price factors):
1. **Cost of production** – higher input costs reduce supply
2. **Technology** – improved technology increases supply
3. **Number of producers** – more sellers = greater market supply
4. **Government policy** – subsidies increase supply; taxes reduce supply
5. **Natural factors** – weather, disasters affect agricultural supply
6. **Producers' expectations** – expected higher future prices may reduce current supply

---

### Concept 3: Market Equilibrium

**Equilibrium**: The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied
- At equilibrium: No shortage, no surplus
- The market "clears"

**Equilibrium Diagram**:
```
Price (₦)
    │        S (Supply)
    │       /
    │      /
 P* │─────●←── Equilibrium point (E)
    │    / \
    │   /   \
    │  /     \ D (Demand)
    │/         \
    └────────────── Quantity
              Q*
```

**Disequilibrium**:
| Situation | Cause | Effect |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| **Excess Demand (Shortage)** | Price below equilibrium | Quantity demanded > quantity supplied → price rises |
| **Excess Supply (Surplus)** | Price above equilibrium | Quantity supplied > quantity demanded → price falls |

---

### Concept 4: Elasticity of Demand and Supply

**Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**:
```
PED = % change in Quantity Demanded ÷ % change in Price

PED = (ΔQ/Q) ÷ (ΔP/P)
```

**Interpreting PED Values**:
| PED Value | Type | Meaning |
|-----------|------|---------|
| PED > 1 | Elastic | Consumers very responsive to price changes (luxury goods) |
| PED < 1 | Inelastic | Consumers less responsive (necessities: salt, petrol) |
| PED = 1 | Unit elastic | % change in Q = % change in P |
| PED = 0 | Perfectly inelastic | Demand unchanged regardless of price (insulin) |
| PED = ∞ | Perfectly elastic | Any price rise → demand falls to zero |

**Factors affecting elasticity**:
- Number of substitutes: More substitutes = more elastic
- Necessity vs Luxury: Necessities are inelastic; luxuries are elastic
- Proportion of income: Expensive items tend to be more elastic
- Time: Demand becomes more elastic over time

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Law of Demand)
**Q**: According to the law of demand, when the price of a commodity rises:

**Options**:
A) Quantity demanded increases
B) Quantity demanded remains the same
C) Quantity demanded decreases ← **CORRECT**
D) Supply increases

**Explanation**:
- Law of Demand: Price ↑ → Quantity Demanded ↓ (inverse relationship)
- This is a movement ALONG the demand curve (not a shift)
- Key phrase: "ceteris paribus" (all other factors held constant)

**Key Concept**: Price change → movement along the curve; Non-price change → shift of the curve

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Equilibrium)
**Q**: Equilibrium price is the price at which:

**Options**:
A) Supply is greater than demand
B) Demand is greater than supply
C) Quantity demanded equals quantity supplied ← **CORRECT**
D) Price is at its highest level

**Explanation**:
- At equilibrium, the market clears: exactly the right amount is produced and sold
- No shortage (excess demand) and no surplus (excess supply)
- This is where the supply curve and demand curve intersect

**Key Concept**: Equilibrium = intersection of supply and demand curves

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Demand Shifters)
**Q**: A rise in consumers' incomes is likely to cause:

**Options**:
A) A decrease in demand for all goods
B) A fall in the price of goods
C) An increase in demand for normal goods ← **CORRECT**
D) A movement along the demand curve

**Explanation**:
- When income rises, people can afford more → demand for normal goods increases
- This shifts the demand curve to the RIGHT (not just a movement along it)
- This applies to NORMAL goods; for inferior goods (e.g., garri), income rise → demand FALLS

**Key Concept**: Income rise → Demand curve shifts right for normal goods

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Elasticity Calculation)
**Q**: The price of rice rises from ₦200 to ₦250 and quantity demanded falls from 100 bags to 80 bags. What is the price elasticity of demand?

**Options**:
A) 0.5
B) 1.0
C) 0.8 ← **CORRECT**
D) 1.25

**Solution**:
```
% change in Q = (100 – 80) / 100 × 100 = 20%
% change in P = (250 – 200) / 200 × 100 = 25%

PED = 20% / 25% = 0.8
```

**Interpretation**: PED = 0.8 < 1 → Inelastic demand (rice is a necessity)

**Key Concept**: PED = % ΔQ ÷ % ΔP; PED < 1 = inelastic

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Combined Demand and Supply)
**Q**: If both demand and supply for a commodity increase simultaneously, the effect on equilibrium will be:

**Options**:
A) Price definitely rises, quantity may rise or fall
B) Price definitely falls, quantity may rise or fall
C) Quantity definitely increases, price may rise or fall ← **CORRECT**
D) Both price and quantity definitely increase

**Explanation**:
- Increase in demand → price up, quantity up
- Increase in supply → price down, quantity up
- Combined: Quantity definitely increases (both forces push quantity up)
- Price effect is uncertain (demand pushes it up, supply pushes it down); the net result depends on which change is larger

**Key Concept**: When demand AND supply both increase, quantity rises, but the price effect is ambiguous

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN ECONOMICS

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Confusing "change in demand" with "change in quantity demanded" | Both involve demand | Price change → movement along curve (quantity demanded); Non-price → shift of curve (demand) |
| Saying all goods are normal goods | Different income responses exist | Normal goods: income↑→demand↑; Inferior goods: income↑→demand↓ |
| Ignoring "ceteris paribus" | Other factors change too | "ceteris paribus" means only the stated variable changes |
| Confusing shortage with deficit | Both mean "not enough" but in different contexts | Shortage = excess demand at a given price; Deficit = government spending > revenue |
| Using absolute values for elasticity when comparing | PED is a ratio | PED < 1 = inelastic; PED > 1 = elastic (compare magnitudes) |

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **Price change → movement ALONG the curve (not a shift)**
- Only non-price factors cause the curve to shift

**Tip 2**: **Equilibrium = where supply curve meets demand curve**
- At P*, Qs = Qd → no surplus, no shortage

**Tip 3**: **Elastic vs Inelastic quick test**
- Ask: Is this a necessity or a luxury?
- Necessity (rice, salt, drugs) → inelastic (PED < 1)
- Luxury (cars, holidays, jewellery) → elastic (PED > 1)

**Tip 4**: **Either/or demand and supply shifts → uncertain price effect**
- When both D and S increase: Q↑ definitely; P = uncertain
- When D increases, S decreases: P↑ definitely; Q = uncertain

**Tip 5**: **Inferior vs Normal goods**
- Normal: Income ↑ → Demand ↑
- Inferior: Income ↑ → Demand ↓ (e.g., second-hand goods, low-quality staples)

## 6. WHAT TO MEMORIZE

**MUST MEMORIZE**:
```
Law of Demand: Price ↑ → Quantity Demanded ↓ (inverse)
Law of Supply: Price ↑ → Quantity Supplied ↑ (direct)

Equilibrium: Qs = Qd (supply = demand)

PED Formula: % change in Qd ÷ % change in Price
  PED > 1 = Elastic (luxury)
  PED < 1 = Inelastic (necessity)
  PED = 0 = Perfectly inelastic
  PED = ∞ = Perfectly elastic

Demand Shifters (Right = increase):
  + Income (normal), + Population, + Substitute price ↑,
  + Tastes favouring good, + Complementary price ↓

Supply Shifters (Right = increase):
  + Technology improves, + Input costs fall, + More producers,
  + Subsidies, + Favourable weather
```
