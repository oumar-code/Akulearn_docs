## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: Types of Comprehension Questions
**Why Important**: Knowing the question type helps you find answers faster

**Types**:
1. **Factual Questions**: "What did X do?" - Answer is directly stated
2. **Inferential Questions**: "Why did X do this?" - Requires reading between lines
3. **Vocabulary Questions**: "What does 'X' mean?" - Need context clues
4. **Main Idea Questions**: "What is the passage mainly about?" - Summary
5. **Tone Questions**: "What is the author's tone?" - Emotional/attitude

**Exam Pattern**: JAMB always includes all 5 types in each comprehension section

---

### Concept 2: Key Reading Strategy (SCAN-READ-ANSWER)

**Step 1: SCAN** (30 seconds)
- Read the questions FIRST (not the passage)
- Underline keywords in questions
- Know what to look for

**Step 2: READ** (1.5-2 minutes)
- Read passage actively
- Circle keywords matching question topics
- Make mental notes of main idea & structure

**Step 3: ANSWER** (1.5-2 minutes)
- Answer questions in order
- Reference specific lines when unsure
- If uncertain, use elimination (cross out wrong answers)

**Total Time per Comprehension**: 5-6 minutes for 3 questions

---

### Concept 3: Context Clues for Vocabulary

**Method 1: Definition Clues**
> "The woman was obsequious (meaning submissive) to her boss."
- Answer: obsequious = submissive

**Method 2: Contrast Clues**
> "Unlike his gregarious brother, John was very solitary."
- Answer: gregarious = sociable (opposite of solitary)

**Method 3: Example Clues**
> "Acrimonious words—biting criticism and angry remarks—filled the room."
- Answer: acrimonious = angry/bitter

**Method 4: Synonym Clues**
> "The ancient artifact was invaluable and precious."
- Answer: invaluable = precious

**Exam Strategy**: 80% of vocabulary questions can be solved with context clues!

## 2. SAMPLE COMPREHENSION PASSAGE (Example)

### Passage: "Technology and Education"
*Approximately 250 words*

The integration of technology into education has transformed how students learn. Digital tools provide immediate access to vast amounts of information, enabling learners to explore topics beyond textbook limitations. However, experts debate whether this abundance of information genuinely improves educational outcomes.

Proponents argue that technology increases engagement through interactive learning platforms, gamification, and personalized learning paths. Students can learn at their own pace, revisiting difficult concepts without the pressure of classroom timing. Furthermore, technology bridges geographical gaps, allowing students in remote areas to access quality education previously unavailable to them.

Conversely, critics worry about technology's negative effects. Excessive screen time may impair concentration and critical thinking skills. Additionally, the digital divide—unequal access to technology—deepens socioeconomic disparities. Students without reliable internet access fall further behind their better-equipped peers.

A balanced approach seems optimal. Technology should complement, not replace, traditional teaching methods. Teachers remain essential for mentoring, motivation, and developing social skills that devices cannot teach. The goal should be integrating technology purposefully, ensuring it enhances learning without replacing human connection.

## 3. SAMPLE COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Question 1 (EASY - Factual)
**Q**: According to the passage, what is ONE advantage of technology in education?

**Options**:
A) It eliminates the need for teachers  
B) It provides access to more information ← **CORRECT**  
C) It guarantees higher exam scores  
D) It reduces the cost of education  

**Explanation**: 
- Line 3-4 states: "Digital tools provide immediate access to vast amounts of information"
- This directly answers the question
- Answer B is explicitly stated in the passage

**Reading Skill**: Factual questions = look for directly stated information

---

### Question 2 (EASY - Vocabulary)
**Q**: What does the word "abundance" mean in this context?

**Options**:
A) Scarcity  
B) Complexity  
C) Large quantity ← **CORRECT**  
D) Digital format  

**Explanation**:
- Sentence: "However, experts debate whether this abundance of information..."
- Context: They're discussing having lots of information from technology
- "Abundance" = many, a lot, large quantity
- Use context clue: "abundance of information" = too much information

**Reading Skill**: Use surrounding words to understand new vocabulary

---

### Question 3 (MEDIUM - Inferential)
**Q**: Why do critics worry about the "digital divide"?

**Options**:
A) Because students spend too much time on screens  
B) Because it creates inequality between students with and without technology ← **CORRECT**  
C) Because teachers are not trained in technology  
D) Because the internet is not secure  

**Explanation**:
- The passage states: "the digital divide—unequal access to technology—deepens socioeconomic disparities"
- And: "Students without reliable internet access fall further behind"
- We must INFER that this creates inequality
- Answer B connects the dots: unequal access → some students disadvantaged

**Reading Skill**: Inferential = connect information to understand underlying meaning

---

### Question 4 (MEDIUM - Main Idea)
**Q**: What is the main idea of the entire passage?

**Options**:
A) Technology is bad for education  
B) Technology solves all educational problems  
C) Technology should be balanced with traditional methods ← **CORRECT**  
D) Teachers are more important than technology  

**Explanation**:
- Paragraph 1: Introduces the debate
- Paragraphs 2-3: Pro and con arguments
- Paragraph 4 (conclusion): "A balanced approach seems optimal"
- The author's main message: Use BOTH technology AND traditional teaching

**Reading Skill**: Main idea is usually in the conclusion or intro

---

### Question 5 (HARD - Tone/Inference)
**Q**: What is the author's tone throughout this passage?

**Options**:
A) Strongly opposed to technology  
B) Enthusiastically supporting technology only  
C) Neutral and balanced ← **CORRECT**  
D) Sarcastic and critical  

**Explanation**:
- Author presents BOTH pros ("Proponents argue...") and cons ("Conversely, critics worry...")
- Uses balanced language: "debate," "both," "balanced approach"
- Doesn't favor one side strongly
- Tone = NEUTRAL, BALANCED, ANALYTICAL
- Key word: "Conversely" = author acknowledges both viewpoints

**Reading Skill**: Tone = author's attitude (positive/negative/neutral/sarcastic)

## 4. COMMON MISTAKES IN COMPREHENSION

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Reading passage first | Wastes time | Read questions FIRST |
| Choosing answers from outside knowledge | Must use passage only | Stick to passage content |
| Missing "not" in questions | Complete misunderstanding | Read question words carefully |
| Confusing factual with inferential | Different question types | Factual = stated; Inferential = implied |
| Picking "sounds right" | Might contradict passage | Verify with passage text |
| Speed-reading and missing details | Misread entire meaning | Read actively, underline keywords |

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **Scan questions first** (1 min)
- Know what you're looking for
- Circle keywords

**Tip 2**: **Read actively** (2 min)
- Underline relevant information
- Mark main ideas & supporting details

**Tip 3**: **Reference the passage** (2 min)
- Never answer from memory
- Always point to specific lines
- Cite evidence: "Line X says..."

**Tip 4**: **Use elimination** (1 min)
- Cross out obviously wrong answers
- Reduces choices from 4 to 2-3
- Guess wisely from remaining options

**Tip 5**: **Time yourself** (5-6 min per section)
- 3 passages × 3-5 questions = 15 min maximum
- If taking longer, practice faster reading

```

"such as" = example coming
```
