## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: What is Electrolysis?

**Definition**: The decomposition of a substance (electrolyte) by passing electric current through it in molten or aqueous form

**Key Components**:
- **Electrolyte**: Compound that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved (e.g., NaCl, CuSO₄, H₂SO₄)
- **Electrodes**: Two conductors dipped into the electrolyte
  - **Anode** (+): Positive electrode → oxidation occurs (loses electrons)
  - **Cathode** (–): Negative electrode → reduction occurs (gains electrons)
- **External power source**: Drives the current (battery or DC supply)

**Memory trick**: **AN OX** = **AN**ode = **OX**idation | **RED CAT** = **RED**uction at **CAT**hode

**Why Important**: 5-7 WAEC/JAMB questions per exam; tested as calculations, electrode products, industrial applications

---

### Concept 2: Products at Electrodes (Discharge of Ions)

**At the Cathode (–)**: Positive ions (cations) are attracted and gain electrons (reduced)
```
For CuSO₄:  Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (copper deposited)
For HCl:    H⁺ + e⁻ → ½H₂ (hydrogen gas released)
For NaCl (aqueous): H⁺ (from water) discharged → H₂ gas
```

**At the Anode (+)**: Negative ions (anions) are attracted and lose electrons (oxidised)
```
For HCl:      Cl⁻ → ½Cl₂ + e⁻ (chlorine gas)
For CuSO₄:   H₂O → ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxygen gas)
For NaCl (aq): Cl⁻ discharged (concentrated) → Cl₂ gas
```

**Discharge Rule** (Which ion is discharged?):
- At cathode: Ion lower in electrochemical series is discharged first (e.g., Cu²⁺ before H⁺)
- At anode: Cl⁻ discharged before OH⁻ if concentration is high

---

### Concept 3: Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

**First Law**: Mass of substance deposited is proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge)
```
m ∝ Q    where Q = It (charge = current × time)
```

**Second Law**: Masses of different substances deposited by the same charge are proportional to their equivalent masses (relative atomic mass ÷ valency)

**Formula for calculations**:
```
m = (Q × M) / (n × F)

Where:
m = mass deposited (grams)
Q = charge (coulombs) = current (A) × time (s)
M = molar mass of element (g/mol)
n = number of electrons transferred (valency)
F = Faraday constant = 96,500 C/mol
```

**Example**:
```
Q: How much copper is deposited when 2A flows for 30 min through CuSO₄?

Q = I × t = 2 × (30 × 60) = 3,600 C
m = (3600 × 64) / (2 × 96500)
m = 230,400 / 193,000
m ≈ 1.19 g of copper deposited
```

### Concept 4: Industrial Applications of Electrolysis

| Application | Electrolyte Used | Product |
|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| Electroplating | Salt of plating metal (e.g., AgNO₃) | Thin metal coating |
| Extraction of aluminium | Molten Al₂O₃ (bauxite) | Aluminium metal |
| Purification of copper | CuSO₄ solution | Pure copper at cathode |
| Production of chlorine | NaCl solution (brine) | Cl₂, H₂, NaOH |
| Anodising aluminium | Dilute H₂SO₄ | Thicker oxide layer |

**Exam Frequency**: Electroplating and chlorine production = VERY HIGH

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Identification)
**Q**: In an electrolytic cell, where does oxidation occur?

**Options**:
A) Cathode
B) Anode ← **CORRECT**
C) Electrolyte
D) Both electrodes

**Explanation**:
- Anode = positive electrode = oxidation (electrons are LOST)
- Cathode = negative electrode = reduction (electrons are GAINED)
- Memory: **AN OX** = Anode = Oxidation

**Key Concept**: Oxidation at anode; reduction at cathode

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Electrode Product)
**Q**: What is produced at the cathode when dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl) is electrolysed?

**Options**:
A) Chlorine gas
B) Oxygen gas
C) Hydrogen gas ← **CORRECT**
D) Sodium metal

**Explanation**:
- HCl in water gives H⁺ and Cl⁻ ions
- Cathode attracts positive ions (H⁺)
- H⁺ + e⁻ → ½H₂↑ (hydrogen gas bubbles)
- At the anode: Cl⁻ → ½Cl₂↑ (chlorine gas)

**Key Concept**: Cathode → positive ions discharged → H⁺ gives H₂

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Application)
**Q**: What is the purpose of electroplating a steel spoon with silver?

**Options**:
A) To increase the spoon's mass
B) To make it harder and heavier
C) To give it an attractive shiny appearance and prevent corrosion ← **CORRECT**
D) To purify the silver

**Explanation**:
- Electroplating coats a cheaper metal with a more expensive/attractive one
- Steel with silver coating: looks better AND resists rust
- The spoon becomes the cathode; silver anode dissolves to provide Ag⁺ ions
- Ag⁺ ions are deposited on the spoon surface

**Key Concept**: Electroplating = decorative AND protective coating

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Faraday Calculation)
**Q**: A current of 5 A is passed through a solution of silver nitrate (AgNO₃) for 965 seconds. What mass of silver is deposited? (Ag = 108 g/mol; F = 96,500 C/mol)

**Options**:
A) 2.7 g
B) 5.4 g ← **CORRECT**
C) 10.8 g
D) 1.08 g

**Solution**:
```
Step 1: Calculate charge Q
Q = I × t = 5 × 965 = 4,825 C

Step 2: Apply Faraday formula
m = (Q × M) / (n × F)
Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag  (n = 1)
m = (4825 × 108) / (1 × 96500)
m = 521,100 / 96,500
m ≈ 5.4 g
```

**Key Concept**: Faraday calculation: m = (Q × M) / (n × F)

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Analysis)
**Q**: When concentrated sodium chloride solution (brine) is electrolysed, the products at the anode and cathode are respectively:

**Options**:
A) Oxygen and hydrogen
B) Chlorine and sodium
C) Chlorine and hydrogen ← **CORRECT**
D) Oxygen and sodium

**Explanation**:
- Brine = concentrated NaCl(aq)
- **At cathode**: H⁺ ions (from water) discharged → H₂ gas
  - (Na⁺ is not discharged because it's high in electrochemical series)
- **At anode**: Cl⁻ ions (concentrated) discharged → Cl₂ gas
  - (Cl⁻ preferred over OH⁻ when concentration is high)
- NaOH is formed in the remaining solution
- Industrial application: Chlor-alkali process

**Key Concept**: Concentrated NaCl → Cl₂ (anode) + H₂ (cathode) + NaOH (solution)

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN ELECTROLYSIS

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Confusing anode (+) and cathode (–) | Mixing up signs | Anode = positive = oxidation; Cathode = negative = reduction |
| Wrong product prediction | Ignoring concentration/electrochemical series | Use discharge rules (concentration, series) |
| Forgetting units in Faraday calculations | Mixing amperes/coulombs/seconds | Q = I (A) × t (s) = coulombs (C) |
| Not counting n (valency) correctly | Using n = 1 for all metals | Cu²⁺ → n = 2; Ag⁺ → n = 1; Al³⁺ → n = 3 |
| Confusing electrolytic with galvanic cells | Both involve electrodes | Electrolytic = external power source; Galvanic = generates power |

## 4. KEY DIAGRAMS (To Understand)

### Diagram 1: Electrolytic Cell Setup
```
     Battery/DC Supply
     (+)        (–)
      |          |
   ANODE      CATHODE
   (oxidation) (reduction)
      |          |
      └──────────┘
         Electrolyte
         (ions conduct electricity)
```

### Diagram 2: Ionic Movement
```
Cations (+) ──────────→ Cathode (–)
Anions  (–) ──────────→ Anode  (+)

At Cathode:  M^n+ + ne⁻ → M (deposited)
At Anode:    X⁻ → X + e⁻  (gas released or electrode dissolves)
```

### Diagram 3: Faraday Formula at a Glance
```
m = (I × t × M) / (n × F)

I = current (A)
t = time (s)
M = molar mass (g/mol)
n = electrons transferred
F = 96,500 C/mol
```

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **AN OX, RED CAT** – Anode = Oxidation; Cathode = Reduction

**Tip 2**: **Electrode product rules**
- Cathode: Metal ions below H in activity series → metal deposited; otherwise H₂ gas
- Anode: Cl⁻ preferred over OH⁻ in concentrated solution; otherwise O₂ gas

**Tip 3**: **Faraday calculation steps**
- Q = I × t (in coulombs)
- m = (Q × M) / (n × F)
- Always check units first!

**Tip 4**: **Electroplating object = cathode**
- Object to be plated is always the cathode
- Plating metal (e.g., silver, gold) is the anode
- Electrolyte = salt solution of plating metal

**Tip 5**: **Industrial applications to remember**
- Brine electrolysis → Cl₂, H₂, NaOH
- Al₂O₃ electrolysis → aluminium extraction
- CuSO₄ electrolysis → copper purification
