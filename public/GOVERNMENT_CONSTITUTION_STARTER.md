## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: The Constitution

**Definition**: The supreme law of a country; the fundamental rules that govern the state, its institutions, and the rights of citizens

**Types of Constitution**:
| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Written** | Codified in a single document | Nigeria, USA |
| **Unwritten (Uncodified)** | Based on conventions, customs, and statutes (not one document) | United Kingdom |
| **Rigid** | Requires a special procedure to amend | Nigeria, USA |
| **Flexible** | Can be amended by ordinary legislative process | United Kingdom |
| **Federal** | Divides power between central and component units | Nigeria, USA |
| **Unitary** | Power concentrated at the centre; components have delegated authority | France, Ghana |

**Sources of the Nigerian Constitution**:
1. Statutes and Acts of the National Assembly
2. Decisions of courts (judicial precedents)
3. Customs and conventions
4. International agreements and treaties

**Supremacy of the Constitution**:
- Any law inconsistent with the constitution is void to the extent of its inconsistency
- Courts have the power of judicial review (declare laws unconstitutional)

**Constitutional Provisions – Nigeria**:
- Section 1: Supremacy of the constitution
- Section 14: Nigeria is a democratic and social state
- Section 36: Right to fair hearing
- Chapter IV: Fundamental Human Rights

---

### Concept 2: Arms of Government

**The Doctrine of Separation of Powers** (Montesquieu):
- Prevents concentration of power in one hand
- Each arm is independent and checks the others

| Arm | Name | Function |
|-----|------|---------|
| **Legislature** | National Assembly (Nigeria) / Congress (USA) | Makes laws |
| **Executive** | President / Governor | Implements laws; governs |
| **Judiciary** | Supreme Court / Courts | Interprets laws; settles disputes |

**Checks and Balances**:
- Legislature checks Executive: Can override presidential veto (2/3 majority), impeach president
- Executive checks Legislature: Presidential veto of bills, dissolving parliament (in some systems)
- Judiciary checks both: Declares laws unconstitutional (judicial review)

**The Nigerian National Assembly**:
| Feature | Senate | House of Representatives |
|---------|--------|------------------------|
| Members | 109 Senators (3 per state + 1 FCT) | 360 members |
| Term | 4 years | 4 years |
| Presided by | Senate President | Speaker |
| Special function | Confirm presidential appointments, ratify treaties | Bills appropriating money must start here |

---

### Concept 3: Legislature – Functions and Types

**Functions of the Legislature**:
1. **Law making** – passes bills into law
2. **Control of finances** – approves annual budget (appropriation bill)
3. **Oversight** – monitors the executive and its agencies
4. **Representation** – represents constituents' interests
5. **Deliberation** – debates national issues
6. **Ratification** – approves treaties and key appointments (Senate)
7. **Impeachment** – can remove president/governor for gross misconduct

**Types of Legislature**:
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Unicameral** | Single chamber | Ghana (Parliament), Tanzania |
| **Bicameral** | Two chambers (upper and lower) | Nigeria (Senate + HoR), USA (Senate + House) |

**How a Bill Becomes Law (Nigeria)**:
```
1. Bill is introduced (First Reading – title only read)
2. Second Reading – general debate on principles
3. Committee Stage – detailed clause-by-clause scrutiny
4. Third Reading – final vote
5. If passed, sent to the other chamber (Senate or HoR)
6. After both chambers pass → President signs into law
7. If President withholds assent → bill returns to NASS
   → 2/3 majority in both chambers overrides veto → becomes law
```

### Concept 4: Electoral Systems

**Election**: A process by which citizens choose their representatives through voting

**Types of Electoral Systems**:

**1. First-Past-the-Post (Simple Plurality)**:
- Winner = candidate with most votes (need not be a majority)
- Used in: Nigeria (for most elections), UK, USA
- Advantage: Simple; produces clear winner
- Disadvantage: Majority of votes can be "wasted"; minor parties underrepresented

**2. Proportional Representation (PR)**:
- Seats distributed in proportion to votes received
- Used in: South Africa, Germany, Israel
- Advantage: Fairer representation of minority parties
- Disadvantage: Often produces coalition governments; can be unstable

**3. Alternative Vote (Preferential Voting)**:
- Voters rank candidates; if no candidate gets 50%+, weakest candidate is eliminated and votes redistributed
- Used in: Australia

**4. Two-Round System (Run-Off)**:
- If no candidate wins outright in round 1, top two candidates contest in round 2
- Used in: France, most Nigerian presidential elections (2/3 of states + majority of votes)

**INEC (Independent National Electoral Commission)**:
- Nigerian body responsible for organizing federal and state elections
- Functions: Register voters, conduct elections, declare results, register political parties

**Requirements for Election in Nigeria (Presidential)**:
- 25 years minimum age (for HoR); 30 years (for Senate); 35 years (President)
- Member of a registered political party
- Educated to at least School Certificate level (for HoR)
- Must win majority of votes AND at least 25% in 2/3 of states (for President)

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Constitution Type)
**Q**: A constitution that can be amended through an ordinary legislative process is described as:

**Options**:
A) Rigid
B) Written
C) Flexible ← **CORRECT**
D) Federal

**Explanation**:
- Flexible constitution = amended by normal parliamentary majority (no special procedure)
- Rigid constitution = requires special procedure (e.g., 2/3 majority or referendum)
- The UK constitution is the classic example of a flexible constitution

**Key Concept**: Flexible = easy to amend; Rigid = requires special/supermajority procedure

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Legislature)
**Q**: The Nigerian National Assembly consists of:

**Options**:
A) The Senate and the Supreme Court
B) The Senate and the House of Representatives ← **CORRECT**
C) The House of Representatives and the Executive
D) The Senate and the State Houses of Assembly

**Explanation**:
- Nigeria operates a bicameral legislature
- National Assembly = Senate (upper house) + House of Representatives (lower house)
- The Supreme Court is the judiciary; State Houses of Assembly are sub-national legislatures

**Key Concept**: National Assembly = Senate + House of Representatives (bicameral)

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Separation of Powers)
**Q**: The power of the legislature to impeach the President is an example of:

**Options**:
A) Separation of powers
B) Checks and balances ← **CORRECT**
C) Judicial review
D) Supremacy of the constitution

**Explanation**:
- Checks and balances = each arm has powers to limit the other arms
- Impeachment is the legislature checking the executive
- Separation of powers = the arms having DISTINCT functions (not overlapping control)
- Judicial review = courts checking legislature/executive by declaring laws unconstitutional

**Key Concept**: Checks and balances = arms limiting each other; Separation = distinct functions

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Electoral System)
**Q**: In Nigeria's presidential election, a candidate must score the highest number of votes AND:

**Options**:
A) Win in all 36 states
B) Win at least 50% of all votes cast
C) Score at least 25% in at least two-thirds of all states ← **CORRECT**
D) Win in at least half of the Local Government Areas

**Explanation**:
- Nigeria uses a two-condition presidential formula (unlike "most votes wins")
- Condition 1: Highest number of votes cast
- Condition 2: At least 25% of votes in at least 2/3 of states (i.e., 24 of 36 states)
- If no candidate meets both, there is a run-off between the top two

**Key Concept**: Nigerian president must win both a national plurality AND 25% in 2/3 of states

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Legislature Process)
**Q**: If the President withholds assent to a bill passed by both chambers of the National Assembly, the bill can still become law if:

**Options**:
A) The Supreme Court declares it constitutional
B) The Senate passes it again by simple majority
C) Both chambers pass it again by at least two-thirds majority ← **CORRECT**
D) INEC certifies the bill through a referendum

**Explanation**:
- Presidential veto in Nigeria can be overridden
- Both chambers must pass the bill AGAIN with a 2/3 supermajority
- This is similar to the US veto override mechanism
- The Supreme Court can review laws but does not enact them

**Key Concept**: Presidential veto override = 2/3 majority in BOTH chambers of NASS

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN GOVERNMENT

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Confusing unicameral and bicameral | "Nigeria has a unicameral legislature" | Nigeria is bicameral (Senate + HoR); Ghana is unicameral |
| Saying judiciary makes laws | All three arms are confused | Legislature = makes laws; Executive = implements; Judiciary = interprets |
| Confusing INEC with NPC | Both are federal bodies | INEC = elections; NPC = National Population Commission (census) |
| Thinking separation of powers means no overlap | Arms share some powers | Checks and balances REQUIRE some overlap to limit each other |
| Stating the President can dissolve NASS | Parliamentary system confusion | Nigeria is presidential; President CANNOT dissolve the legislature |

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **Bicameral vs Unicameral – know African examples**
- Nigeria, South Africa, Kenya → bicameral
- Ghana, Tanzania, Senegal → unicameral

**Tip 2**: **Senate = 109; House of Representatives = 360 (Nigeria)**
- 3 senators per state × 36 states = 108 + 1 for FCT = 109

**Tip 3**: **Presidential veto override = 2/3 majority in BOTH chambers**
- Not a simple majority; requires supermajority

**Tip 4**: **INEC functions: Register voters, conduct elections, register parties**
- NOT the body to declare which candidate is qualified to run (that's the courts)

**Tip 5**: **Rigid constitution = special procedure to amend; Flexible = ordinary procedure**
- Nigeria and USA have rigid constitutions; UK has a flexible constitution
