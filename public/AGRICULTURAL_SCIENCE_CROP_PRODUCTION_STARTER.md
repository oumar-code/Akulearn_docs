## 1. KEY CONCEPTS (Master These)

### Concept 1: Soil Science

**Definition**: Soil is the uppermost layer of the Earth's surface that supports plant growth, composed of mineral particles, organic matter, water, air, and living organisms

**Components of Soil**:
| Component | Proportion (Ideal) | Description |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Mineral particles | ~45% | Weathered rock fragments (sand, silt, clay) |
| Organic matter (humus) | ~5% | Decomposed plant and animal remains |
| Water | ~25% | Held in pore spaces; carries dissolved nutrients |
| Air | ~25% | Provides oxygen for root respiration and soil organisms |

**Soil Texture** – based on particle size:
| Type | Particle Size | Properties |
|------|-------------|------------|
| Sand | Large (>0.05 mm) | Coarse, gritty, drains quickly, low fertility |
| Silt | Medium | Smooth, moderately fertile, moderate drainage |
| Clay | Small (<0.002 mm) | Sticky, smooth when wet, slow drainage, high nutrient retention |
| Loam | Mixed | Ideal for farming; good drainage AND water retention |

**Soil Profile** (Layers from top to bottom):
```
A Horizon (Topsoil): Darkest; richest in humus and nutrients; where most plant roots grow
B Horizon (Subsoil): Lighter colour; less organic matter; some minerals washed down from A
C Horizon (Parent Material): Partially weathered rock
D Horizon (Bedrock): Solid unweathered rock
```

**Soil Formation Factors** (PORCT):
- **Parent material** – type of rock determines mineral composition
- **Organisms** – earthworms, bacteria decompose organic matter
- **Relief** – slope affects erosion and water retention
- **Climate** – rainfall and temperature drive weathering
- **Time** – longer = deeper, more developed soil

**Soil pH**:
```
pH 1–6   = Acidic soil  (most crops prefer pH 6–7)
pH 7     = Neutral
pH 8–14  = Alkaline/Basic

Too acidic → Liming (add CaO, Ca(OH)₂, or CaCO₃) to raise pH
Too alkaline → Add sulphur or organic matter to lower pH
```

### Concept 2: Nutrients and Fertilisers

**Essential Plant Nutrients**:

**Macronutrients** (needed in large quantities):
| Nutrient | Symbol | Function | Deficiency Symptom |
|---------|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Nitrogen | N | Leaf growth, protein synthesis | Yellowing (chlorosis) of leaves |
| Phosphorus | P | Root development, energy transfer | Purple/red leaves, poor roots |
| Potassium | K | Fruit ripening, disease resistance | Leaf edge burning, weak stems |
| Calcium | Ca | Cell wall formation | Distorted young leaves, tip burn |
| Magnesium | Mg | Chlorophyll production | Yellowing between leaf veins |

**Micronutrients** (needed in small quantities): Iron, Zinc, Boron, Copper, Manganese

**Types of Fertilisers**:
| Type | Examples | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|------|---------|-----------|--------------|
| **Organic** | Compost, Manure, Bone meal | Improves soil structure, slow-release, cheap | Slow-acting, low nutrient concentration |
| **Inorganic (Chemical)** | NPK, Urea, SSP | Fast-acting, concentrated, precise nutrient ratio | Can degrade soil structure, leaching, expensive |

**NPK Fertiliser**: Most commonly used compound fertiliser
- N (Nitrogen): leaf/stem growth
- P (Phosphorus): root/flower/fruit
- K (Potassium): overall health, fruit quality

**NPK Ratio Example**: NPK 15-15-15 means 15% N, 15% P₂O₅, 15% K₂O

### Concept 3: Crop Production – Planting and Cultivation

**Classification of Crops**:
| Classification | Basis | Examples |
|---------------|-------|---------|
| Cereal crops | Grain crops from grass family | Maize, Rice, Sorghum, Millet, Wheat |
| Legumes | Pod-bearing; fix nitrogen | Cowpea, Groundnut, Soybean |
| Root/Tuber crops | Underground storage | Cassava, Yam, Cocoyam, Sweet potato |
| Vegetable crops | Leaf, stem, or fruit eaten | Tomato, Spinach, Okra, Carrot |
| Tree crops (Plantation) | Permanent perennial crops | Cocoa, Oil palm, Rubber, Coffee |
| Fibre crops | Used for textile | Cotton, Jute, Sisal |

**Crop Improvement Techniques**:
- **Hybridisation**: Crossing two different varieties to produce a hybrid with better characteristics
- **Introduction**: Bringing a new crop variety from outside the region
- **Selection**: Choosing and breeding plants with desirable traits
- **Mutation breeding**: Using radiation/chemicals to induce genetic changes

**Methods of Propagation**:
| Method | Description | Examples |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Seed (sexual)** | Planting seeds | Maize, rice, groundnut |
| **Vegetative (asexual)** | Using plant parts | Cuttings (cassava), suckers (banana), grafting (mango, citrus), budding |

### Concept 4: Farming Systems and Practices

**Major Farming Systems in Nigeria**:

| System | Description | Example Crops |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| **Subsistence farming** | Small scale; grow for family consumption | Cassava, yam, vegetables |
| **Commercial farming** | Large scale; grow for profit/export | Cocoa, groundnut, rubber |
| **Mixed cropping** | Growing two or more crops together simultaneously | Maize + cassava |
| **Crop rotation** | Planting different crops in sequence on the same land | Maize → Cowpea → Sorghum |
| **Shifting cultivation** | Move to new land after old land is exhausted | Common in rainforest areas |
| **Mono-culture** | Growing only one crop on a piece of land | Large cocoa plantations |

**Advantages of Crop Rotation**:
1. Reduces soil depletion (different crops use different nutrients)
2. Legumes fix nitrogen into the soil
3. Reduces pest and disease build-up
4. Reduces need for chemical fertilisers

**Land Preparation**:
```
Clearing → Primary tillage (deep ploughing) → Secondary tillage (harrowing/raking)
→ Ridging or bed formation → Planting
```

**Common Farm Pests and Their Control**:
| Pest | Crops Affected | Control Method |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| Weevils | Stored grain | Insecticide, hermetic storage, smoke |
| Army worm | Maize | Pesticide, biological control |
| Aphids | Vegetables, legumes | Insecticide, neem extract |
| Termites | Cassava, root crops | Chemical treatment of soil |

## 2. SAMPLE PROBLEMS (5 Increasing Difficulty)

### Problem 1 (EASY – Soil Types)
**Q**: Which soil type is best suited for crop farming due to its ideal water retention and drainage properties?

**Options**:
A) Sandy soil
B) Clay soil
C) Loam soil ← **CORRECT**
D) Silt soil

**Explanation**:
- Loam = mixed sand, silt, and clay → best of all properties
- Sandy soil drains too fast → nutrients leach out quickly
- Clay soil retains too much water → waterlogging, poor aeration
- Loam is the farmer's ideal because it holds moisture AND drains well

**Key Concept**: Loam soil = ideal for farming (sand + silt + clay)

---

### Problem 2 (EASY – Plant Nutrients)
**Q**: A crop showing yellowing of leaves is most likely deficient in:

**Options**:
A) Phosphorus
B) Potassium
C) Nitrogen ← **CORRECT**
D) Calcium

**Explanation**:
- Nitrogen deficiency = yellowing (chlorosis) of leaves, starting with older leaves
- Phosphorus deficiency = purplish/reddish leaves, poor root development
- Potassium deficiency = scorched/brown leaf edges
- Calcium deficiency = distorted young leaves

**Key Concept**: Yellow leaves = Nitrogen deficiency

---

### Problem 3 (MEDIUM – Crop Classification)
**Q**: Cassava, yam, and sweet potato are classified as:

**Options**:
A) Cereal crops
B) Legume crops
C) Root and tuber crops ← **CORRECT**
D) Fibre crops

**Explanation**:
- All three store food underground in roots or tubers
- Cereal crops produce seeds/grain (maize, rice)
- Legumes produce pods with seeds (groundnut, cowpea)
- Fibre crops provide fibres for textiles (cotton)

**Key Concept**: Underground storage organs → root/tuber crops

---

### Problem 4 (MEDIUM – Crop Rotation)
**Q**: One major advantage of crop rotation is that it:

**Options**:
A) Requires more fertiliser each year
B) Encourages the growth of one crop type
C) Reduces pest and disease build-up in the soil ← **CORRECT**
D) Prevents the use of machinery

**Explanation**:
- Crop rotation changes the host plant for pests and diseases each season
- Pests that target maize cannot survive if cowpea is planted next
- Also: legumes in the rotation fix nitrogen, reducing fertiliser needs
- Mono-culture (same crop always) INCREASES pest build-up

**Key Concept**: Crop rotation breaks pest cycles, fixes nitrogen, and maintains soil fertility

---

### Problem 5 (HARD – Soil Improvement)
**Q**: A farmer has soil with a pH of 4.5. Which amendment should be applied?

**Options**:
A) Sulphur
B) Ammonium sulphate
C) Lime (CaCO₃) ← **CORRECT**
D) NPK fertiliser

**Explanation**:
- pH 4.5 is acidic (below 6.0 is too acidic for most crops)
- To RAISE pH (reduce acidity) → apply LIME (CaCO₃, CaO, or Ca(OH)₂)
- Sulphur LOWERS pH (used when soil is too alkaline)
- Ammonium sulphate is acidifying → makes soil MORE acidic
- Most crops thrive at pH 6.0–7.0

**Key Concept**: Acidic soil → add lime to raise pH; Alkaline soil → add sulphur to lower pH

## 3. COMMON MISTAKES IN AGRICULTURAL SCIENCE

| Mistake | Why Wrong | Correct Understanding |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Saying sandy soil is best for farming | Sandy soil drains too fast | Loam soil is best; sandy soil loses nutrients quickly |
| Confusing nitrogen and phosphorus deficiency | Both affect growth | Nitrogen → yellow leaves; Phosphorus → purple/red leaves |
| Saying legumes need nitrogen fertiliser | Legumes FIX nitrogen | Legumes have root nodules with Rhizobium bacteria that fix atmospheric N₂ |
| Mixing up cereal and legume crops | Both produce food seeds | Cereals = grass family grains (maize, rice); Legumes = pod-bearing (groundnut, cowpea) |
| Thinking crop rotation means planting the same crop in a different field | Rotation = different crops in sequence | Rotation = different CROPS on the SAME land each season |

```

Year 4: Cassava or Yam (root crop; different nutrient needs)
→ Back to Year 1: Soil nitrogen restored!
```

## 5. EXAM QUICK TIPS

**Tip 1**: **Loam = ideal soil; remember it as the "Goldilocks" soil**
- Not too sandy, not too clayey → just right for most crops

**Tip 2**: **N = Nitrogen = Yellow (nitrogen deficiency = yellowing)**
- N is for leaves; P is for roots; K is for fruit/disease resistance

**Tip 3**: **Legumes fix nitrogen – this is the key advantage of crop rotation**
- Cowpea, groundnut, soybean all have Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules

**Tip 4**: **pH too low (acidic) → add lime; pH too high (alkaline) → add sulphur**
- "L for Lime = L for Low acidity = raise pH"

**Tip 5**: **Vegetative propagation: Cassava = stem cuttings; Banana = suckers; Yam = sets**
- These crops do NOT need seeds to propagate
