### 3.1: Essay Structure and Organization

*Duration: 22 minutes*

**The Five-Paragraph Essay Structure**

The basic essay has three parts: **Introduction, Body, Conclusion**.

**INTRODUCTION (1 paragraph)**
- **Hook**: Grab reader's attention (question, quote, interesting fact, anecdote)
- **Background**: Provide context, define key terms
- **Thesis Statement**: Main argument or point (last sentence of intro)

Example Intro:
*Hook:* "What makes a great leader?"
*Background:* "Throughout history, leaders have shaped nations. In Nigeria, leaders like Dr. Nnamdi Azikiwe fought for independence."
*Thesis:* "A great leader possesses vision, integrity, and the ability to inspire others."

**BODY (3 paragraphs, one for each main point)**

Each body paragraph follows **TEEL/PEEL structure**:
- **T (Topic Sentence)**: Introduces paragraph's main idea (supports thesis)
- **E (Explanation)**: Explains the point in detail
- **E (Evidence/Example)**: Provides specific examples, facts, quotes
- **L (Link)**: Connects back to thesis or transitions to next paragraph

Example Body Paragraph:
*Topic Sentence:* "First, a great leader must have clear vision."
*Explanation:* "Vision means seeing the future and setting goals to achieve it."
*Evidence:* "Dr. Azikiwe envisioned an independent Nigeria and worked tirelessly toward that goal."
*Link:* "Without vision, a leader cannot guide a nation forward."

**CONCLUSION (1 paragraph)**
- **Restate Thesis**: Summarize main argument (in different words)
- **Summarize Key Points**: Briefly recap three main ideas
- **Closing Statement**: Final thought, call to action, or broader implication

Example Conclusion:
"In conclusion, effective leadership requires vision, integrity, and inspiration. These qualities enable leaders like Azikiwe to transform nations. As future leaders, we must cultivate these traits to build a better Nigeria."

**Thesis Statement**

A thesis statement:
- States main argument/claim
- Appears at end of introduction
- Guides entire essay
- Is specific and debatable (not obvious fact)

**Good Thesis**: "The Nigerian government should invest more in renewable energy to combat climate change and create jobs."
**Weak Thesis**: "Climate change is bad." (Too vague, not debatable)

**Topic Sentences**

Topic sentences:
- Begin each body paragraph
- State paragraph's main idea
- Support thesis statement
- Preview what paragraph will discuss

**Transitions**

Transition words connect ideas smoothly:
- **Addition**: furthermore, moreover, additionally, also
- **Contrast**: however, on the other hand, nevertheless, although
- **Example**: for instance, for example, specifically
- **Sequence**: first, second, finally, next, then
- **Conclusion**: in conclusion, therefore, thus, consequently

**Coherence and Unity**

**Coherence**: Ideas flow logically; sentences connect smoothly
**Unity**: All sentences in paragraph support topic sentence; all paragraphs support thesis

**Nigerian Context**: When writing about Nigerian issues (education, economy, culture), organize ideas clearly. For WAEC exams, five-paragraph structure provides solid framework.


### 3.2: Essay Types and Writing Techniques

*Duration: 23 minutes*

**1. NARRATIVE ESSAY**

Tells a story from personal experience or imagination.

**Features**:
- First or third person
- Chronological order (time sequence)
- Vivid descriptions and dialogue
- Shows character development
- Has plot (beginning, middle, end)

**Structure**:
- **Intro**: Set scene, introduce characters/conflict
- **Body**: Tell story events in order, build to climax
- **Conclusion**: Resolve conflict, reflect on significance

**Example Topic**: "A Day I Will Never Forget"
*Narrative*: Describe a memorable experience (winning competition, moving to new city, family event). Use descriptive language and show emotions.

**2. DESCRIPTIVE ESSAY**

Describes person, place, object, or event using sensory details.

**Features**:
- Appeals to five senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch)
- Uses figurative language (similes, metaphors)
- Creates vivid mental picture
- Organized spatially (top to bottom, near to far) or by importance

**Example Topic**: "The Lagos Market"
*Description*: "The market buzzes with energy. Vendors shout prices, hawkers weave through crowds. The aroma of roasted plantain mingles with fresh fish. Colorful fabrics—ankara, adire—cascade from stalls. The air is thick, humid, alive with commerce."

**3. EXPOSITORY ESSAY**

Explains or informs about a topic objectively.

**Features**:
- Third person
- Presents facts, not opinions
- Clear, straightforward language
- Logical organization

**Types**:
- **Process**: Explains how to do something ("How to Prepare Jollof Rice")
- **Cause-Effect**: Shows reasons and results ("Effects of Deforestation in Nigeria")
- **Compare-Contrast**: Examines similarities/differences ("Traditional vs. Modern Education")
- **Definition**: Explains a concept ("What is Democracy?")

**Example Topic**: "The Nigerian Educational System"
*Exposition*: Explain structure (primary, secondary, tertiary), curriculum, challenges, reforms. Use facts and statistics.

**4. ARGUMENTATIVE/PERSUASIVE ESSAY**

Persuades reader to accept a viewpoint or take action.

**Features**:
- Clear thesis stating position
- Logical arguments supported by evidence (facts, statistics, expert quotes)
- Addresses counterarguments (opposing views)
- Appeals to logic (logos), emotion (pathos), credibility (ethos)

**Structure**:
- **Intro**: State issue and thesis (your position)
- **Body Para 1-2**: Present arguments with evidence
- **Body Para 3**: Address counterargument and refute
- **Conclusion**: Restate position, call to action

**Example Topic**: "Should School Uniforms Be Mandatory?"
*Argument*: Take position (yes or no), provide reasons (promotes equality, reduces distractions, OR limits self-expression, costly for families), support with evidence, refute opposing view.

**Developing Paragraphs**

**Show, Don't Tell**: Use specific details, not vague statements.
- **Weak**: "The teacher was nice."
- **Strong**: "The teacher smiled warmly, patiently explaining the math problem for the third time."

**Use Examples**: Concrete examples make abstract ideas clear.
- Claim: "Education improves lives."
- Example: "In rural Kano, a literacy program enabled women to start small businesses, lifting families out of poverty."

**Nigerian Context**: WAEC often asks for essays on Nigerian topics (leadership, education, environment, culture). Practice all four types. Narrative/descriptive suit creative writing; expository/argumentative suit formal topics.


### 3.3: Formal Writing: Letters, Reports, and Articles

*Duration: 22 minutes*

**FORMAL LETTER WRITING**

**Format**:

```
Sender's Address
[Your full address]

Date
[Month Day, Year]

Recipient's Name and Address
[Title, Name]
[Organization]
[Address]

Salutation
Dear Sir/Madam, OR Dear Mr./Mrs./Dr. [Last Name],

Body
[Paragraph 1: State purpose]
[Paragraph 2-3: Provide details]
[Paragraph 4: Closing/action requested]

Complimentary Close
Yours faithfully, (if 'Dear Sir/Madam')
Yours sincerely, (if named recipient)

Signature
[Your handwritten signature]
[Your typed name]
```

**Types of Formal Letters**:

1. **Application Letter** (job/school admission)
   - State position applied for
   - Highlight qualifications
   - Request interview

2. **Complaint Letter**
   - Describe problem clearly
   - State desired resolution
   - Provide contact info

3. **Request Letter**
   - Explain what you need
   - Give reasons
   - Thank recipient

**Example Formal Letter**:

12 Ibrahim Taiwo Street
Ikeja, Lagos

October 15, 2024

The Principal
King's College
Lagos Island, Lagos

Dear Sir,

**Application for Admission into SS1**

I am writing to apply for admission into Senior Secondary 1 at King's College for the 2024/2025 academic session.

I completed Junior Secondary School at Heritage Academy with excellent grades in Mathematics, English, and Science. I am a dedicated student with strong leadership skills, having served as class prefect.

I have attached my academic transcripts, birth certificate, and letters of recommendation. I would be grateful for the opportunity to attend your esteemed institution.

Thank you for considering my application. I look forward to your positive response.

Yours faithfully,

Adebayo Johnson

---

**REPORT WRITING**

A report presents information objectively and formally.

**Structure**:

1. **Title**: Clear, specific (e.g., "Report on School Science Fair")
2. **Introduction**: Purpose, background, scope
3. **Findings/Body**: Present information (use headings, subheadings, bullet points)
4. **Conclusion**: Summarize key points
5. **Recommendations**: Suggest actions (if applicable)

**Example Report**:

**Report on Library Usage at Heritage Academy**

**Introduction**: This report examines library usage by SS1 students during Term 1 (January-March 2024). Data was collected through surveys and records.

**Findings**:
- 60% of students visit library weekly
- Most popular section: Fiction (45%)
- Main challenge: Limited opening hours

**Conclusion**: Library is well-used but could serve students better with extended hours.

**Recommendations**: Increase opening hours from 4pm to 6pm on weekdays.

---

**ARTICLE WRITING**

An article informs, entertains, or persuades on a topic for publication (newspaper, magazine, blog).

**Structure**:
1. **Headline**: Catchy, summarizes topic
2. **Introduction**: Hook reader, state topic
3. **Body**: Develop ideas with paragraphs (use subheadings if needed)
4. **Conclusion**: Summarize, final thought

**Example Article Headline**:
"Five Study Tips Every Nigerian Student Should Know"

**Tone**: Can be formal or semi-formal (more relaxed than letter/report)

**Nigerian Context**: Practice writing formal letters for school applications, complaint letters to service providers, reports for school projects, articles for school magazines on Nigerian issues.


### 3.4: Punctuation, Grammar, and Editing

*Duration: 23 minutes*

**ESSENTIAL PUNCTUATION**

**1. PERIOD (.)**
- Ends declarative sentence: "Lagos is Nigeria's commercial capital."
- After abbreviations: Dr., Mr., Mrs., etc.

**2. COMMA (,)**
- Separates items in list: "I bought rice, beans, and plantain."
- After introductory phrase: "After school, I went home."
- Sets off nonessential info: "My brother, who lives in Abuja, is visiting."
- Between independent clauses with conjunction: "I studied hard, and I passed."

**3. SEMICOLON (;)**
- Links closely related independent clauses: "I love reading; it expands my knowledge."
- Separates complex list items: "We visited Lagos, Nigeria; Accra, Ghana; and Dakar, Senegal."

**4. COLON (:)**
- Introduces list: "Buy these items: bread, milk, eggs."
- Introduces explanation: "I have one goal: to succeed."

**5. APOSTROPHE (')**
- Shows possession: "John's book," "the students' uniforms" (plural possessive)
- Contractions: "don't" (do not), "it's" (it is)
- Note: "its" (possessive) vs. "it's" (it is)

**6. QUOTATION MARKS (" ")**
- Direct speech: She said, "I am going to school."
- Titles of short works: "The Voter" (short story)

**7. QUESTION MARK (?) and EXCLAMATION MARK (!)**
- Questions: "Where is the library?"
- Exclamations: "What a beautiful day!"

**COMMON GRAMMAR ERRORS**

**1. Sentence Fragments**
- **Incorrect**: "Because I was tired." (incomplete thought)
- **Correct**: "I slept early because I was tired."

**2. Run-on Sentences**
- **Incorrect**: "I studied hard I passed the exam." (two sentences joined incorrectly)
- **Correct**: "I studied hard, and I passed the exam." (add conjunction + comma)
- **Or**: "I studied hard. I passed the exam." (two sentences)
- **Or**: "I studied hard; I passed the exam." (semicolon)

**3. Subject-Verb Agreement (see Lesson 1)**
- **Incorrect**: "The students is coming."
- **Correct**: "The students are coming."

**4. Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement**
- Pronoun must agree with noun it replaces
- **Incorrect**: "Each student must bring their book."
- **Correct**: "Each student must bring his or her book." OR "Students must bring their books."

**THE WRITING PROCESS**

**1. PREWRITING**
- Brainstorm ideas
- Choose topic
- Research (if needed)
- Outline main points

**2. DRAFTING**
- Write first draft quickly
- Focus on getting ideas down
- Don't worry about perfection

**3. REVISING**
- Improve content and organization
- Add details, remove irrelevant info
- Check thesis/topic sentences
- Strengthen transitions

**4. EDITING**
- Fix grammar, spelling, punctuation
- Check sentence structure
- Ensure consistent verb tense

**5. PROOFREADING**
- Final check for errors
- Read aloud to catch mistakes
- Check formatting

**Revision Checklist**:
✓ Clear thesis statement?
✓ Topic sentences support thesis?
✓ Paragraphs well-developed with examples?
✓ Smooth transitions?
✓ Strong conclusion?
✓ Grammar and punctuation correct?
✓ No spelling errors?

**Nigerian Context**: In WAEC exams, grammar and punctuation affect your score. Practice editing your essays. Common errors: mixing British/American spelling (favour vs. favor—use British in Nigeria), incorrect apostrophes, run-on sentences.


## Worked Examples


### Example 3.1: Writing a Thesis Statement

**Context**: Essay topic: "The Importance of Education in Nigeria"

**Problem**: Write a strong thesis statement for this essay.

**Solution**:
```
Strong thesis: 'Education is the key to Nigeria's development, as it empowers individuals, drives economic growth, and fosters national unity.' This thesis is specific, debatable, and previews three main points (empowerment, economy, unity) that body paragraphs will cover.
```

**Skills Tested**: Thesis formulation, Essay planning, Critical thinking


### Example 3.2: Developing a Body Paragraph (TEEL)

**Context**: Essay thesis: 'Social media has both positive and negative effects on Nigerian youth.'

**Problem**: Write one body paragraph about a positive effect using TEEL structure.

**Solution**:
```
**T** (Topic): Social media enables Nigerian youth to connect and collaborate globally. **E** (Explanation): Platforms like Twitter and Instagram allow young Nigerians to network with peers worldwide, share ideas, and learn about different cultures. **E** (Evidence): For example, Nigerian tech entrepreneurs use Twitter to connect with investors and mentors abroad, leading to successful startups. **L** (Link): Thus, social media serves as a valuable tool for global engagement and opportunity creation. [This paragraph is unified, coherent, and supports the thesis.]
```

**Skills Tested**: Paragraph development, TEEL structure, Use of evidence


### Example 3.3: Correcting Punctuation Errors

**Context**: Sentence: "My friend who lives in Kano is visiting we are going to the market"

**Problem**: Correct punctuation errors.

**Solution**:
```
Corrected: 'My friend, who lives in Kano, is visiting. We are going to the market.' OR 'My friend, who lives in Kano, is visiting; we are going to the market.' Explanations: (1) Commas set off nonessential clause 'who lives in Kano.' (2) Run-on sentence fixed with period or semicolon.
```

**Skills Tested**: Comma usage, Fixing run-on sentences, Punctuation


## Practice Problems

**3.1.B**: What are the three main parts of an essay?

> **Answer**: Introduction, Body, Conclusion.

> **Explanation**: Basic essay structure.


**3.2.B**: Where does the thesis statement appear?

> **Answer**: At the end of the introduction paragraph.

> **Explanation**: Thesis guides the essay and comes last in intro.


**3.3.B**: What does TEEL stand for?

> **Answer**: Topic sentence, Explanation, Evidence/Example, Link.

> **Explanation**: Paragraph structure acronym.


### Core Level


**3.4.C**: Write a topic sentence for a body paragraph about 'honesty is important in leadership.'

> **Answer**: Example: 'Honesty is essential for leaders because it builds trust with followers.'

> **Explanation**: Topic sentence states paragraph's main idea and links to thesis.


**3.5.C**: Identify essay type: 'Tell about your most memorable vacation.'

> **Answer**: Narrative essay (tells personal story).

> **Explanation**: Narrative = story from experience.


**3.6.C**: Correct punctuation: 'I need to buy eggs milk and bread'

> **Answer**: 'I need to buy eggs, milk, and bread.'

> **Explanation**: Commas separate items in list.


**3.7.C**: What is the complimentary close if you start with 'Dear Sir/Madam'?

> **Answer**: 'Yours faithfully,'

> **Explanation**: Formal letter convention: 'faithfully' for unnamed recipient, 'sincerely' for named.


### Challenge Level


**3.8.CH**: Write a short argumentative thesis about 'school uniforms in Nigeria.' Take a clear position.

> **Answer**: Example: 'School uniforms should be mandatory in Nigerian schools because they promote equality, enhance discipline, and foster school identity.' OR opposite position with reasons.

> **Explanation**: Argumentative thesis states debatable position with supporting reasons.


**3.9.CH**: Edit for errors: 'The students grades was excellent they studied very hard for there exams'

> **Answer**: 'The students' grades were excellent. They studied very hard for their exams.' OR '...excellent; they studied...'

> **Explanation**: Corrections: students' (possessive plural), were (subject-verb agreement), period/semicolon (run-on), their (correct spelling).


**3.10.CH**: Outline a five-paragraph essay on 'The Benefits of Reading' including intro (with thesis), three body paragraph topics, and conclusion.

> **Answer**: Intro: Hook (quote), background (importance of reading), Thesis: 'Reading benefits students by expanding knowledge, improving language skills, and enhancing creativity.' Body 1: Expands knowledge. Body 2: Improves language. Body 3: Enhances creativity. Conclusion: Restate thesis, summarize benefits, closing thought on lifelong learning.

> **Explanation**: Outline shows clear structure with thesis guiding body paragraphs.


## Glossary


**Thesis statement**: Main argument or claim of an essay, stated in introduction.

- *Example*: 'Education is essential for national development.'


**Topic sentence**: First sentence of body paragraph stating its main idea.

- *Example*: Opens paragraph and supports thesis.


**Transition**: Word or phrase connecting ideas smoothly.

- *Example*: However, furthermore, in conclusion.


**Coherence**: Logical flow of ideas in writing.

- *Example*: Sentences and paragraphs connect clearly.


**Unity**: All parts of writing relate to main idea.

- *Example*: Every sentence supports topic sentence/thesis.


**Narrative essay**: Essay that tells a story.

- *Example*: Personal experience or fictional tale.


**Expository essay**: Essay that explains or informs objectively.

- *Example*: Process, cause-effect, compare-contrast.


**Argumentative essay**: Essay that persuades reader to accept a position.

- *Example*: Uses evidence to support claim.


**Formal letter**: Professional letter following specific format.

- *Example*: Application, complaint, request letters.


**TEEL**: Paragraph structure: Topic, Explanation, Evidence, Link.

- *Example*: Framework for body paragraphs.

### WAEC Exam-Style Questions


**1. Write a five-paragraph essay (400-450 words) on 'The Role of Youth in Nation Building.' Include clear thesis, three body paragraphs with TEEL structure, and strong conclusion. Use Nigerian examples.**

> **Answer Guide**: Intro with thesis (e.g., 'Nigerian youth play crucial role through education, innovation, and civic engagement'). Body 1: Education (youth pursuing studies to develop skills). Body 2: Innovation (young entrepreneurs creating solutions). Body 3: Civic engagement (voting, community service). Conclusion restating thesis and calling youth to action. **2. Write a formal letter to the Chairman of your Local Government Area requesting renovation of your school library. Include proper format, clear problem statement, and polite request.**

> **Answer Guide**: Use full formal letter format (addresses, date, salutation, body, close, signature). State problem (dilapidated library affecting learning), provide specific details (broken furniture, leaking roof), request action (renovation with budget allocation), express gratitude. Use 'Yours faithfully,' if 'Dear Sir/Madam.'
